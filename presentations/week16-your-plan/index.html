Healthy Relationships

üíú

Relationships shape your life

Romantic partners, friendships, family, work colleagues ‚Äî all your relationships affect your wellbeing. Learning to build healthy ones now sets you up for life.

‚öñÔ∏è

Healthy relationships are equal

Both people treat each other with respect. Both people's feelings, opinions, and boundaries matter. Nobody is "in charge" of the other person.

üó£Ô∏è

Communication is everything

Healthy relationships involve honest, open communication. You can disagree without disrespect. You listen to each other and work through problems together.

üîí

Trust and honesty go both ways

You trust each other because you're both honest. You don't hide things or lie. Trust is earned through consistent, reliable behaviour from both partners.

Green Flags & Red Flags

These apply to everyone ‚Äî regardless of gender

üü¢ Green Flags

  • They respect your boundaries
  • They're happy when good things happen to you
  • They apologise when they're wrong
  • They encourage your friendships and interests
  • You feel comfortable being yourself
  • They listen without dismissing your feelings
  • You trust each other with your phones/passwords
  • Disagreements don't turn into personal attacks

üî¥ Red Flags

  • They put you down or make you feel small
  • They're jealous of your friends or family
  • They try to control what you wear, do, or who you see
  • They blame you for their behaviour
  • They check your phone or social media constantly
  • You feel like you're walking on eggshells
  • They pressure you to do things you don't want to do
  • They make threats or use intimidation

Anyone ‚Äî of any gender ‚Äî can show these behaviours. Trust your instincts.

Boundaries & Consent

üõë

Everyone has boundaries

Physical, emotional, digital, and social boundaries. Healthy relationships respect all of them. "No" is a complete sentence ‚Äî you don't need to justify your boundaries.

‚úÖ

Consent is ongoing and enthusiastic

Consent isn't just about physical intimacy ‚Äî it applies to sharing photos, borrowing things, making plans. A "yes" today isn't a "yes" forever. Check in with each other.

üí¨

It's okay to speak up

If something doesn't feel right, say so. A good partner will listen. If they dismiss your concerns, guilt-trip you, or get angry ‚Äî that's a red flag.

ü§ù

Both people are responsible

In a healthy relationship, both people take responsibility for their actions. If something goes wrong, you work it out together ‚Äî not blame each other.

When Things Go Wrong

Unhealthy ‚â† Abusive (but can become it)

Some relationships are unhealthy but not abusive ‚Äî poor communication, different values, growing apart. These can often be fixed with effort.

But some cross into harmful territory. Abuse can be physical, emotional, financial, or digital. It can happen to anyone regardless of gender, age, or background.

Signs you might need help:

  • You're scared of their reaction
  • You've changed who you are to please them
  • Your friends/family are concerned
  • You feel trapped or isolated

Stress and Burnout

üò¥

Sleep issues

Can't fall asleep, or sleeping too much. Feeling tired no matter how much you rest. Sleep problems are often the first sign something's off.

üò§

Irritability and mood changes

Snapping at people, feeling overwhelmed by small things, or losing interest in things you used to enjoy. It's not just "being moody" ‚Äî it's a sign you need support.

üîã

Exhaustion and feeling empty

Physical and emotional exhaustion that doesn't go away with rest. Feeling like you're running on empty. Burnout is real, and it's preventable.

üß†

Difficulty concentrating

Can't focus, forgetting things, struggling to make decisions. Your brain is telling you it needs a break.

If you're experiencing these signs, reach out. It's not weakness ‚Äî it's self-awareness.

Work-Life Balance

‚öñÔ∏è

Start building good habits now

Don't wait until you burn out to learn balance. Build it into your routine now. Set boundaries early in your career ‚Äî don't let work consume your life.

üìµ

Learn to switch off

When you're done for the day, be done. Don't check work emails at midnight. Don't study until 2am every night. Rest is productive.

üéØ

Prioritise what matters

You can't do everything. Learn to say no. Focus on what's important, not just what's urgent. Your health and relationships matter more than perfect grades or overtime.

üßò

Downtime isn't laziness

Rest, hobbies, time with friends ‚Äî these aren't distractions. They're essential. You're not a machine. Recharge.

Balance isn't something you find ‚Äî it's something you create.

Imposter Syndrome

üé≠

What is imposter syndrome?

Feeling like a fraud, even when you're qualified. "Everyone else knows what they're doing, but I'm just winging it." Spoiler: everyone feels this way sometimes.

üÜï

It's worse in new situations

First day at uni, first job, first time doing anything ‚Äî imposter syndrome kicks in hard. That's normal. You're not supposed to know everything on day one.

üë•

Everyone feels it (even experts)

Doctors, engineers, artists, tradies ‚Äî everyone has moments of self-doubt. The difference is they keep going anyway. Confidence comes from doing, not from feeling ready.

üí¨

Talk about it

Share how you're feeling with friends, classmates, or mentors. You'll find out they feel the same way. Normalising imposter syndrome takes away its power.

Feeling like an imposter doesn't mean you are one. It means you're growing.

Healthy Habits for Adulting

üò¥

Sleep ‚Äî seriously, prioritise it

7-9 hours per night. Not negotiable. Your brain needs sleep to function. "I'll sleep when I'm dead" is terrible advice. Sleep now, live better.

üèÉ

Exercise ‚Äî move your body

You don't need a gym membership. Walk, run, swim, play sport, lift weights, dance ‚Äî whatever gets you moving. Exercise reduces stress and improves mood.

üë´

Social connection ‚Äî don't isolate

Spend time with people who make you feel good. Call your mates. Join clubs. Loneliness is a real issue for young adults ‚Äî stay connected.

üç∫

Limit alcohol ‚Äî it's not a coping tool

Drinking to deal with stress or anxiety makes things worse, not better. Enjoy responsibly, but don't rely on it. If you're using alcohol to cope, talk to someone.

Build these habits now. Future you will thank you.

Building Your Network

üîó

Why networking matters

Most jobs aren't advertised publicly. 80% of jobs are filled through connections. Networking isn't schmoozing ‚Äî it's building genuine relationships with people in your field.

üëî

Start with people you know

Teachers, coaches, family friends, past employers. They're your first network. Let them know what you're interested in. Ask for advice, not just jobs.

üéì

Join clubs, groups, and communities

Industry groups, uni clubs, online communities (Discord, Reddit, Slack channels). Show up, contribute, help others. Networking is about giving, not just taking.

üí¨

Be curious and genuine

Ask people about their work, how they got started, what they love about it. People love talking about themselves. Listen more than you talk.

Your network is your net worth. Start building it now.

LinkedIn Basics

üì∏

Professional profile photo

Not a selfie. Not a party photo. Just a clear, friendly headshot. Dress like you would for a job interview. Smile.

üìù

Write a simple headline

Don't just write "Student". Try "Year 12 Graduate | Aspiring Software Developer" or "Apprentice Electrician | Denmark, WA". Make it clear who you are and what you're aiming for.

üéØ

Add your experience and skills

List jobs, volunteer work, school projects, skills you've learned. Even if it's retail or hospitality ‚Äî it counts. Highlight achievements, not just duties.

üîó

Connect with people thoughtfully

Don't spam connection requests. Add a note: "Hi, I'm a Year 12 student interested in [field]. I'd love to connect and learn from your experience."

Your LinkedIn is your professional presence. Make it count.

Finding Mentors

üßë‚Äçüè´

What is a mentor?

Someone with experience in your field who can give advice, share insights, and help you navigate challenges. Not a boss ‚Äî a guide.

üéØ

Who to ask

Teachers, past employers, family friends, people you meet at events or online. Look for people 5-10 years ahead of you in your career path.

üí¨

How to ask

Be direct but respectful: "I really admire your work in [field]. Would you be open to a coffee chat or occasional advice as I start my career?" Most people are flattered and happy to help.

‚è±Ô∏è

What to expect

Mentorship isn't a formal thing. It's occasional check-ins, advice when you need it, introductions to others. Respect their time. Come prepared with questions.

Mentors open doors you didn't know existed.

Professional References

üìû

What are references?

People who can vouch for your work ethic, skills, and character. Employers call them to verify you're as good as you say you are.

üë§

Who to use as references

Past employers, teachers, coaches, volunteer coordinators. Not family or friends. Pick people who can speak to your professionalism and skills.

ü§ù

Maintain relationships

Stay in touch with people who can be references. Send a quick email update once a year. Thank them when they help you. Don't just reach out when you need something.

‚úÖ

Always ask permission first

Before listing someone as a reference, ask them. Give them context on the role you're applying for. Make it easy for them to say yes.

References matter. Treat people well, and they'll speak well of you.

December ‚Äî You're free!

The Hidden Job Market

70%

of jobs are never advertised publicly

They're filled through referrals, networking, and internal hires

Why networking matters

  • Employers trust referrals more than strangers
  • You hear about roles before they're posted
  • Someone can vouch for your character
  • Builds relationships for your career

How to network (for real)

  • Tell family/friends you're job hunting
  • Connect with people on LinkedIn
  • Attend industry events/meetups
  • Join professional groups on socials
  • Ask for informational interviews

Applicant Tracking Systems

A robot reads your resume before a human does

What is an ATS?

Software that scans resumes for keywords, qualifications, and formatting. It ranks candidates automatically. If your resume isn't ATS-friendly, it might never reach a human recruiter.

ATS scans for

Keywords from job description, required skills, job titles, education, certifications, years of experience

ATS rejects

Complex formatting, images/logos, tables, headers/footers, unusual fonts, graphics and charts

Reality check: 75% of resumes never make it past the ATS. You need to optimise for both robots AND humans.

Resume Basics

What to include

Contact info

Name, phone, email, LinkedIn (if you have one), location (city/suburb)

Professional summary

2-3 sentences about who you are and what you're looking for

Work experience

Job title, company, dates, bullet points of achievements (not just duties)

Education

School name, qualifications, expected completion date

Skills

Relevant technical and soft skills (match the job description)

What to skip

Photo of yourself

Not standard in Australia (unless acting/modelling)

Age or date of birth

Illegal to discriminate, so don't include it

Hobbies (usually)

Only if directly relevant to the role

"References available on request"

They'll ask if they need them

Length: 1-2 pages max. For school leavers, 1 page is fine.

Beating the ATS

How to make sure your resume gets read by a human

Use keywords

Copy exact phrases from the job ad. If they want "customer service", use those exact words.

Simple formatting

Standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, Times). No tables, text boxes, or columns.

Standard sections

Use headings like "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills" - not creative names.

Spell out acronyms

Write "Bachelor of Science (BSc)" not just "BSc".

File format

.docx or PDF (check what they ask for). Name it: FirstName_LastName_Resume.pdf

Tailor every time

Customize your resume for each job. Copy-paste won't cut it.

Pro tip: AI tools like ChatGPT can help optimize your resume for ATS, but don't let them write it for you. Your experience and voice need to be authentic.

Cover Letters

Still needed in 2026?

When to write one

  • When it's specifically requested
  • For competitive roles (many applicants)
  • When you need to explain something (career change, gap in work)
  • When you have a connection to the company
  • For professional/corporate roles

When to skip it

  • Casual/retail jobs (unless asked)
  • When the application doesn't have a field for it
  • If you're applying to many jobs quickly
  • When instructions say "resume only"

The 3-paragraph structure

Para 1

Why you're excited about THIS company/role

Para 2

What you bring (2-3 relevant achievements)

Para 3

Call to action (keen to discuss further)

Warning: Don't just regurgitate your resume. A cover letter should tell a story about why you're a great fit.

Video Interviews

The new normal in 2026

Technical setup

Lighting

Face a window or use a lamp in front of you. Avoid backlighting.

Background

Clean, neutral wall. Remove distractions. Blur if needed.

Camera angle

Eye level. Stack books under laptop if needed.

Audio

Use headphones/earbuds to avoid echo. Test beforehand.

Interview tips

Eye contact

Look at the camera, not the screen. Feels more natural.

Dress code

Same as in-person. Yes, wear proper pants.

Notes nearby

Keep key points on sticky notes around your screen. Don't read verbatim.

Test run

Do a practice call with a friend. Check everything works.

Pro tip: Some companies use AI to analyze your video interviews (facial expressions, word choice). Be yourself, but be professional.

What Employers Actually Want

It's not always about technical skills

1. Reliability

Show up on time. Do what you say you'll do. Follow through. This beats talent every time for entry-level roles.

2. Positive attitude

Willingness to help, team player, doesn't complain constantly. No one wants to work with someone who brings the vibe down.

3. Willingness to learn

You won't know everything - that's fine. What matters is being coachable and asking questions when you don't understand.

4. Communication skills

Can you write a clear email? Talk to customers/colleagues respectfully? Listen properly? Hugely underrated skill.

5. Problem-solving

When something goes wrong, do you panic or figure it out? Can you think on your feet?

Remember

For your first job, employers expect to train you. They're hiring for potential and character, not perfection.

References

Who to ask and how to prepare them

Good references

  • Previous managers/supervisors
  • Teachers (if first job)
  • Sports coaches (if relevant)
  • Volunteer coordinators
  • Family friends (if professional)
How many? Usually 2-3 references. Have them ready before you apply.

Avoid using

  • Family members (parents, siblings)
  • Friends your own age
  • People who don't know your work
  • Anyone who might give a lukewarm reference
Always ask first! Don't list someone as a reference without permission.

How to prepare your references

  • Ask if they're comfortable being a reference (give them an out)
  • Tell them what jobs you're applying for
  • Remind them of your achievements/projects together
  • Give them a heads up when you've listed them
  • Thank them afterwards (text/email is fine)

Know Your Rights

Australian minimum wage & Fair Work basics

$24.10

per hour (casual)

National Minimum Wage as of July 2024 (+ casual loading)

$20.08

per hour (permanent)

Permanent/part-time rate (but you get leave entitlements)

Actual rates may be higher depending on your industry award (retail, hospitality, etc.)

Your rights include

Fair pay

At least minimum wage for your age/industry. Check Fair Work website.

Safe workplace

Employer must provide safe environment, training, and equipment.

Breaks

Rest breaks during shifts (usually 30min unpaid for 5+ hour shift).

Payslips

Must be provided within 1 day of pay. Keep them all.

Super (if you earn $450+/month)

Employer pays 11.5% on top of your wage into super fund.

Protection from discrimination

Can't be discriminated against for age, gender, race, disability, etc.

Check your pay and rights:

www.fairwork.gov.au

Your First Week

What to expect and how to make a good impression

What to expect

  • Paperwork (TFN, super, bank details)
  • Workplace induction/safety training
  • Meeting lots of new people (you'll forget names, that's normal)
  • Feeling overwhelmed and confused
  • Making mistakes (everyone does)
  • Not being very productive yet

How to impress

  • Show up 5-10 minutes early
  • Ask questions when you don't understand
  • Take notes (bring a notebook)
  • Offer to help teammates
  • Put your phone away during work
  • Say thank you when people help you

Things that will get you fired quickly

Don't

Show up late without notice

Don't

Be on your phone constantly

Don't

Complain about everything

Don't

Ignore safety rules

Don't

Gossip about coworkers

Don't

Lie or make excuses

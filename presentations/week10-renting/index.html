The True Cost of Moving Out

It's way more than just rent

Reality Check

Most people only think about rent. But that's just the beginning. Moving out requires thousands upfront and hundreds more every week.

$3,000+ Upfront costs before you move in
$800-1,200 Weekly expenses (living alone)
$45k+ Annual income needed (minimum)

What you'll actually need money for

  • Bond (4 weeks rent held by Bond Administrator)
  • Rent in advance (usually 2 weeks)
  • Utilities connection fees (power, water, internet)
  • Furniture and household items (unless fully furnished)
  • Moving costs (truck hire, helpers)
  • Ongoing weekly expenses (rent, bills, food, transport)

The Employment Factor

Regional WA can save you $100-200/week on rent, but you need to secure employment first. Many regional jobs are in mining, agriculture, healthcare, or tourism. Research job availability before committing.

Mining Towns (FIFO)

Karratha, Port Hedland, Newman often have mining company-provided accommodation or higher wages ($80k-120k+) that offset high rental costs ($600-900/wk). FIFO (Fly-In-Fly-Out) work means living in Perth but working regional.

Bond and Upfront Costs

The money you need BEFORE moving in

Sticker Shock Warning

You can't just move in with one week's rent. Most landlords require 4 weeks bond + 2 weeks rent in advance. That's 6 weeks total upfront.

Example: $550/week apartment

Bond (4 weeks, held by Bond Administrator WA) $2,200
Rent in advance (2 weeks) $1,100
Power connection + deposit $150-300
Water (if not included) $0-100
Internet connection + modem $100-200
Moving truck hire $150-400
TOTAL UPFRONT $3,700-4,300

About the Bond

Your bond is lodged with the Bond Administrator (government agency). The landlord can't just keep it. You get it back at the end if you leave the place clean and undamaged. It usually takes 2-3 weeks to get refunded.

The Savings Reality

Living in a share house saves approximately $21,000 per year compared to living alone. That's a house deposit in 3-4 years, or a new car, or a European holiday every year.

Share housing isn't just for students. Many young professionals choose it to save aggressively for their goals while still living independently.

What Landlords Want

How to actually get approved for a rental

Competitive Market

With vacancy rates under 1% in Perth, landlords can be very selective. You're competing with 20-50+ applicants for every property. You need to stand out.

Essential Documents

  • Photo ID - Driver's licence or passport
  • Proof of income - Recent payslips (last 3 months) or employment contract
  • Rental history - Previous landlord references, rental ledger showing on-time payments
  • References - Character references (employer, teacher, coach)
  • Bank statements - Show you can afford it (3 months)
  • Credit check consent - They'll check your credit score

What They're Looking For

  • Income 3x rent - $575/wk rent = need $1,725/wk income
  • Stable employment - Permanent/ongoing contract preferred
  • Clean rental history - No evictions, no damage, no missed rent
  • Good credit score - No defaults, bankruptcies, or late payments
  • Non-smoker - Most rentals are now smoke-free
  • No pets - Unless specified as pet-friendly (rare)

First-Time Renter Tips

If you don't have rental history

  • Get a guarantor (parent/guardian co-signs)
  • Offer extra weeks rent in advance
  • Provide stellar character references
  • Show strong employment and savings
  • Consider share houses (easier to get in)

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Gaps in employment history
  • Bad credit score
  • Can't provide references
  • Income too low for rent
  • Poor presentation at inspection
  • Incomplete application

The Application Process

1. Attend open inspection | 2. Submit application within 24 hours | 3. Landlord reviews (1-7 days) | 4. If approved, sign lease and pay bond/rent | 5. Move in on agreed date. Speed matters - apply immediately if you like the place.

Your Rights as a Renter

Know the law in Western Australia

Bond Protection

  • Bond must be lodged with Bond Administrator WA within 14 days
  • Landlord cannot keep your bond without valid reason (damage, unpaid rent, cleaning)
  • You have the right to be present at final inspection
  • Disputes go to Magistrates Court if you can't agree
  • Get a condition report at move-in (document existing damage with photos)

Landlord Must Provide

  • Property in clean, working condition
  • Working locks on doors/windows
  • Working smoke alarms
  • Safe electrical and plumbing
  • 24-48 hours notice for inspections
  • Privacy (can't just walk in)
  • Timely repairs (safety issues = 24hrs)

You Must Provide

  • Rent on time (weekly/fortnightly/monthly)
  • Keep property reasonably clean
  • Report damage and needed repairs
  • Allow inspections (with notice)
  • No illegal activity on premises
  • Don't damage property intentionally
  • Give proper notice when leaving

Notice Periods (Western Australia)

Fixed-term lease ending (you leaving) 30 days
Periodic lease (you leaving) 21 days
Landlord ending periodic lease (no reason) 60 days
Landlord ending for breach (e.g., unpaid rent) 7 days

Get Help

Consumer Protection WA: 1300 304 054 | Tenancy WA: Free advice for renters | Community Legal Centres: Free legal help if you can't afford a lawyer

Common Scams

Never pay bond/rent before seeing the property in person. Never pay via Western Union/Bitcoin/gift cards. Always get receipts. If it seems too good to be true (cheap rent in great area), it's probably a scam.

Living at Home Longer

It's not a failure, it's strategic saving

The Smart Move

More young Australians are living at home into their 20s - not because they're "failures," but because they're financially savvy. It's a legitimate wealth-building strategy.

That's a house deposit, or a business startup, or financial security.

Make it Work for Everyone

Contribute Fairly

  • Pay board ($100-200/week is reasonable)
  • Buy your own groceries/toiletries
  • Do household chores without being asked
  • Respect house rules and curfews (even if you're 20+)
  • Keep your space clean

Build Your Future

  • Save aggressively (aim for 50%+ of income)
  • Build your credit score (small credit card, paid in full)
  • Develop life skills (cooking, budgeting, maintenance)
  • Set a target goal and timeline
  • Have an exit plan (don't coast forever)

When Living at Home Makes Sense

Studying full-time | Saving for house deposit | Starting a business | Paying off debt | Building emergency fund | Career uncertainty | First 1-2 years of entry-level job

When It's Time to Move Out

You've hit your savings goal | Family conflict affecting mental health | You need independence for personal growth | Commute is killing you | You're 25+ and coasting | Job opportunity elsewhere

Summary

  • Landlords want proof of income, references, rental history
  • Your bond must be lodged with Bond Administrator
  • Living at home longer = smart wealth building

Action Steps

  • Calculate your actual income vs expenses
  • Research real rent prices in your target area
  • Build an emergency fund (3-6 months expenses)
  • Start building credit responsibly
  • Get rental references lined up
  • Practice budgeting now (50/30/20 rule)
  • Learn to cook and clean (seriously)
  • Have honest money conversations with family
